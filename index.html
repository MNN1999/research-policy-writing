Brief 1 ‚Äî Making sustainability reporting decision‚Äëuseful (without overselling it)

Audience: boards, reporting teams, regulators, standard-setters, investors

Sustainability reporting is often treated as if disclosure alone will resolve sustainability problems. A more realistic theory of change is: disclosure can improve investor decision-making and managerial attention‚Äîyet it cannot, on its own, internalize externalities.

What the evidence says

  • Sustainability-related financial information is distinct from financial statements because it is often prospective, may refer to resources not owned by the reporting entity, and includes non-monetary information‚Äîyet it shares the objective of providing material information to investors.
  • Corporations do not generally have economic self-interest (even long-term) in eliminating external costs. Where societal interests diverge from investor interests, reporting that targets investors alone is incomplete and requires public policy.

Implications

  • For reporting teams: integrate sustainability-related financial disclosure into mainstream management reporting, not as an ‚Äúadd-on‚Äù.
  • For boards: treat sustainability disclosures as part of strategy, risk, and capital allocation governance‚Äîthen ask what sits outside disclosure.
  • For policy: pair standards with incentives and regulation that actually shift externalities (pricing, liability, procurement, permitting).

Suggested next actions

  1. Map which sustainability issues are financially material vs. broader stakeholder materiality‚Äîand be transparent about the boundary.
  2. Document a short ‚Äútheory of change‚Äù for disclosures: who uses them, for what decision, and what behaviour should change.
  3. Prioritise a small number of decision-critical metrics (transition plans, dependencies, and scenario sensitivity), then build quality controls.

Source: Barker (2025) ‚Äî Corporate sustainability reporting

Brief 2 ‚Äî AI for sustainable development: move from ‚Äúrisks‚Äù to people‚Äôs choices

Audience: universities, research centers, innovation agencies, policymakers, civil society

The dominant AI narrative is framed around arms races and risk management. A human development lens asks a different question: what choices can be made so AI expands people‚Äôs freedoms and agency.

What the evidence says

  • A global survey cited in the Human Development Report suggests AI use is already substantial (~20% of respondents) and is expected to rise rapidly across HDI groups.
  • Whether AI erodes or amplifies agency depends on how it is designed and implemented; delegating some decisions can increase agency, while high-stakes automated decisionmaking can create severe ‚Äúunfreedoms‚Äù.
  • The Report identifies three action areas: building a complementarity economy, driving innovation with intent, and investing in capabilities.

Implications for a research center (Misum / HoSS)

  • Use AI to augment research and outreach work (synthesis, translation, dataset exploration), with strong quality assurance.
  • Design events and outputs that keep human agency central: who has decision rights, who bears risks, and what accountability exists.
  • Invest in ‚Äúcapabilities that count‚Äù: training researchers and stakeholders in AI literacy, bias awareness, and responsible deployment.

Suggested next actions

  1. Create an ‚ÄúAI + Sustainable Markets‚Äù seminar series focused on real decisions (employment, credit, procurement, transition planning).
  2. Publish a short guide for researchers: when AI speeds up work vs. when it harms rigor (transparency, reproducibility, citation discipline).
  3. Prototype an Impact Lab use case that is pro-worker and pro-citizen (e.g., tools that expand choices rather than replace them).

Source: UNDP (2025) ‚Äî Human Development Report: A matter of choice

Brief 3 ‚Äî Sustainable markets need institutional plumbing: three lessons from microfinance, innovation, and ocean partnerships

Audience: practitioners, development finance, policymakers, partnership builders

Sustainable market transformation often fails for a simple reason: incentives and institutions do not match the mission. Three bodies of evidence highlight how institutional design shapes outcomes.

1) Transformation changes business models

When microfinance institutions transform from NGOs to shareholder-owned and often regulated entities, portfolio yields fall while operational expenses decrease, alongside increases in deposits and commercial leverage.

2) Democracy and human capital underpin innovation

Cross-country evidence links democratization to higher technology indicators (patents and related measures), with human capital mechanisms (education, media access, broader middle classes) emphasized as key channels.

3) Partnerships don‚Äôt happen automatically

Blue economy research shows growing interest but limited Africa‚ÄìIndia collaboration; proposed leverage points include socio-cultural, economic, institutional, environmental, and scientific dimensions.

So what?

  • Expect trade-offs: ‚Äúprofessionalization‚Äù can improve efficiency while raising mission drift risks (e.g., rising average loan size).
  • Build institutions: governance, transparency, and accountability are not side issues‚Äîthey are the delivery mechanism.
  • Design partnerships: shared resources (like the Indian Ocean) need shared rules, shared data, and shared learning loops.

Suggested next actions

  1. When scaling sustainability initiatives: define the mission, the incentives, and the ‚Äúred lines‚Äù (what you won‚Äôt trade off).
  2. Measure what matters: go beyond proxies; use multidimensional indicators (not just loan size, not just publication counts).
  3. Make collaboration easier than non-collaboration: shared calls, shared datasets, and clear partnership governance.

Sources: D‚ÄôEspallier et al. (2016) ¬∑ Coccia (2010) ¬∑ Matovu et al. (2025)

Corporate sustainability reporting

Citation: Richard Barker (2025), Journal of Accounting and Public Policy.

Read the summary

Research question

What is sustainability reporting, what form should it take, and what difference can it make?

Core argument

  • Define sustainability through Brundtland + SDGs; highlight relevance to natural resources and human rights.
  • Distinguish ‚Äúsustainability-related financial information‚Äù from broader stakeholder reporting.
  • Disclosure can improve decisions, but externalities typically require public policy; corporations cannot be assumed to eliminate them voluntarily.

Why it matters

For sustainable markets, reporting is infrastructure: it can improve capital allocation and governance, but it must be paired with incentives and regulation if society expects real externality reduction.

Limitations

Conceptual paper; value is in framing and policy implications rather than new empirical results.

From NGOs to banks: how institutional transformation reshapes microfinance

Citation: D‚ÄôEspallier, Goedecke, Hudon & Mersland (2016), World Development.

Read the summary

Research question

When MFIs transform into shareholder-owned and often regulated entities, how does their business model change?

Method

Event study on 66 transformed MFIs, comparing business model components before vs. after transformation.

Key findings

  • Portfolio yield decreases (lower interest rates), while operational expenses decline.
  • Commercial debt leverage and deposits increase; funding costs fluctuate less.
  • Average loan size rises (often used as a mission-drift proxy); ROA dips in the short term while ROE improves in the medium/long run.

Why it matters

It‚Äôs a concrete example of how ‚Äúprofessionalization‚Äù can improve efficiency while shifting incentives. For sustainable finance, it highlights the need for governance and impact measurement beyond single proxies.

Democratization as a driver of technological and economic change

Citation: Mario Coccia (2010), Technological Forecasting & Social Change.

Read the summary

Research question

How is democratization related to innovation and economic change across countries?

Approach

Cross-country analysis using democracy indices (Freedom House, Polity, Vanhanen) and innovation proxies (patents, royalties, R&D, researchers).

Key findings

  • Most free countries show substantially higher innovation indicators than least free countries.
  • Models estimate a positive effect of democratization on technology measures, with robustness checks across alternative innovation proxies.
  • Human capital mechanisms (education, media access, broader middle classes) are highlighted as key channels.

Why it matters

Sustainable markets need innovation that diffuses widely. This paper strengthens the case for institution- and human-capital-aware sustainability strategies (not purely technological ones).

Blue economy partnerships: Africa‚ÄìIndia and the Indian Ocean

Citation: Matovu, Mim & Lutalo (2025), Sustainable Futures.

Read the summary

Research question

Where and how can Africa and India leverage blue economy opportunities to drive sustainable development and partnerships?

Approach

Bibliometric analysis of 1,712 Scopus documents + perspective synthesis.

Key findings

  • Blue economy research has grown since 2012; India‚Äôs annual publication growth rate exceeds Africa‚Äôs, but citations are declining.
  • Africa‚ÄìIndia collaboration is limited; most collaborations are with developed nations.
  • The authors propose five leverage points: socio-cultural, economic, institutional, environmental, and scientific.

Why it matters

Partnerships are an implementation technology. This review offers practical ‚Äúlevers‚Äù for building collaborations that move beyond publications toward delivery.

Human Development Report 2025: A matter of choice

Citation: UNDP (2025), People and possibilities in the age of AI.

Read the summary

Core question

What choices should shape the AI era so it advances (rather than undermines) human development?

Key messages

  • Move beyond ‚Äúraces and risks‚Äù to people‚Äôs possibilities and agency.
  • AI can amplify agency when it broadens informed choices‚Äîbut can erode agency in high-stakes, opaque decision contexts.
  • Three priorities: complementarity economy, innovation with intent, and investing in capabilities that count.

Why it matters

For sustainability work, AI is a cross-cutting enabler and risk amplifier. The report provides a high-level framework for responsible deployment that can be translated into research agendas, events, and policy dialogue.

MSc thesis: economic complexity, human development, and SDGs 14/15

Citation: Numan & Kundu (2024), Lund University (DABE).

Read the summary

Research question

How do economic complexity (ECI) and human development (HDI) relate to environmental indicators for life below water and life on land?

Approach

Machine learning models (linear, spline, random forest, neural networks) + SHAP interpretability across SDG 14/15 indicators.

Key findings

  • Random Forest performs especially well for SDG 15 indicators; SHAP highlights ECI as a top predictor for forest cover/biomass.
  • Evidence consistent with EKC-style non-linearities (U-shaped / inverted-U relationships in SHAP dependence plots).
  • For SDG 14, effects vary by indicator (notable impacts for chlorophyll and sustainable fisheries), highlighting trade-offs and context dependence.

Why it matters

This is a practical example of ‚ÄúImpact Lab‚Äù style work: translating complex empirical results into interpretable insights and policy-relevant questions.
